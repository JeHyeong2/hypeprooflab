A headline from last week's Harvard Business Review caught my eye: "AI Is Upending Marketing on Two Fronts," by professor Stefano Puntoni. Normally, such dramatic framing comes with a healthy dose of hyperbole. Not this time. Looking at the news that dropped in the past week alone, "two fronts" might actually be an understatement.

The first front is the transformation of how consumers find information. The era of typing keywords into Google and scanning ten blue links is already fading. Perplexity's newly unveiled "Computer" platform captures the pace of this shift. The company calls it a "general-purpose digital worker" — a system where multiple sub-agents collaborate to reason, search, remember, code, and deliver results. When a consumer asks for "the best budget wireless earbuds," this agent doesn't serve up a list of sponsored links. It scrapes reviews, compares prices, factors in usage patterns, and presents a synthesized answer. Traditional search advertising barely has room to breathe.

In the same week, Anthropic rolled out a major update to Claude Cowork, connecting it with Google Workspace, Docusign, WordPress, and other enterprise tools for multi-step task automation. Google added real transactional capabilities to Gemini — ordering food, hailing rides. Viewed individually, these are interesting product updates. Viewed together, they reveal a pattern: AI has crossed the line from providing information to taking action.

This is the second front. The entity making purchasing decisions is changing. For marketers, this should send a chill down the spine. Traditional marketing targets human psychology — scarcity effects, social proof, anchoring, framing. These tools, refined over decades, all assume human cognitive biases as their foundation. But what happens when a significant portion of purchase decisions are delegated to AI agents? An AI doesn't flinch at "Only 3 left in stock." It has no emotional response to an Instagram influencer's endorsement.

In psychological terms, we might call this a "persuasion target shift." Persuading a human and persuading an algorithm are entirely different games. If SEO was the art of persuading Google's algorithm, the marketing of tomorrow must persuade hundreds of AI agents simultaneously. Each uses a different model, evaluates content by different criteria, and arrives at "purchase recommendation" through a different chain of reasoning.

Here's where an interesting paradox emerges. The most effective way to persuade AI agents might simply be to build genuinely good products. With humans, brand imagery, emotional marketing, and viral campaigns could create a perception that exceeded actual product quality. AI agents, however, read spec sheets, run sentiment analysis on real user reviews, and compare price-to-performance ratios numerically. A market where bluster doesn't work may be opening up.

Of course, reality won't be this clean. First, AI agents carry biases from their training data. A model trained on data where certain brands appear more frequently may recommend those brands disproportionately — leaving room for a new breed of "AI agent optimization." Second, humans remain the end consumers. Even if an AI recommends a product, a person still clicks the buy button and lives with the experience. The power of emotion and narrative won't vanish entirely.

The marketing of tomorrow therefore demands a dual strategy. One track is the traditional craft of moving human hearts through storytelling and branding. The other is structured data that AI agents can evaluate — verifiable quality metrics, transparent product information, machine-readable specifications. The brands that master both tracks will be the ones that survive.

To borrow HBR's framing, we stand in the middle of two concurrent revolutions. Perplexity Computer is calling itself a "digital worker." Claude Cowork is handling tasks across Excel and PowerPoint. Gemini is ordering your lunch. This world has already begun. The question left for marketers is stark: does your brand carry a value proposition that can be articulated to an AI agent? Or has it been riding entirely on human cognitive biases? The answer may determine the next five years of survival.
