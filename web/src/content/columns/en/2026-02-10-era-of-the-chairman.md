## Chapter 1: The Economy of Eyes Fades Away

For the past 20 years, the digital economy has revolved around one simple formula: gather users' attention and sell it to advertisers. Google did it, Meta did it, Naver and Kakao did it.

Dwell time, DAU, retention, click-through rate—these flashy metrics were ultimately variations on a single question: "How long and how much of human attention did we capture?"

But the moment agents take over execution, the premise of this formula collapses. Because the eyes to show things to disappear.

Agents don't view ads. Even if they do, they're optimized to ignore them. We're shifting from a world where ten options were spread before humans to choose from, to one where an agent selects just one and executes immediately. The era of screen share is ending, and the era of execution share is beginning.

This doesn't simply mean that advertising formats are changing. It means that the global digital advertising market worth $600 billion is losing its reason for existence. It's not the format of advertising that's shaken—it's the very premise of advertising itself.

## Chapter 3: Tear Down the Walls or Be Forgotten Inside the Castle?

Here lies the most practical fear of existing businesses. And this is an exact replay of the innovator's dilemma that Clayton Christensen described.

App-based services have walls built up over years. Member databases, payment information, behavioral data—this was the lock-in, this was the moat. Until now, trapping users inside this Walled Garden has been the core of competitive strategy.

But if you open doors based on MCP and APIs, agents will freely move across multiple services. The moat gets filled in. Conversely, if you don't open the doors? Agents can't access you at all. Your very existence vanishes.

Open up and the moat crumbles; stay closed and you disappear from the world. This is the dilemma.

And ultimately, economic gravity determines the answer. The cost of marketing to agents is structurally cheaper than marketing to human users. Not many companies can resist this gravity. They'll inevitably be pulled toward openness.

Except that in the midst of this transition, small companies born API-first from the start have a structural advantage—that's the truly frightening story for existing giants.

## Chapter 5: The Planet Where a Day Becomes a Month

Consider the decision-making cycles of existing companies—quarterly strategy reviews, annual roadmaps, semi-annual budget planning—all of this is completely out of sync with the pace of change.

The judgment that "adoption rates are still low" is based on a linear diffusion model. However, agent adoption is likely to spread non-linearly—exponentially—through the combination of network effects and zero-marginal-cost structures.

The moment a killer agent gets pre-installed on Android once, the shift where "installing apps to use services" itself becomes a relic of the past could take just a few months.

When a company that planned to review quarterly opens its eyes next quarter, the outside world may have already aged several years. Like Cooper returning from Miller's planet.

## Prologue: A 60-Year-Old Promise Breaks

Since Douglas Engelbart invented the mouse in 1963, there has been an implicit promise between humans and computers.

"If humans learn the machine's language, the machine will help with human work."

We learned CLI, mastered GUI, acquired touch, and even learned voice commands. Icons, menus, buttons—all of this was ultimately humans taking half a step toward machines.

But now, that promise is breaking. The machine is walking toward humans first. It interprets human intent and completes execution on its own, machine to machine. This isn't about "becoming more convenient." It's a quiet yet violent extinction, where the intermediary layer called interface loses its reason for existence.

And when the interface disappears, all the castles built upon it crumble together.


## Chapter 2: The War for the New Throne Begins

Here an uncomfortable question arises. If an agent selects the one optimal solution that fits the context, who designs the criteria for that choice?

This is precisely where a new form of power is born.

In the search era, Google reigned as the one who decided "what to show at the top." In the agent era, that power shifts to model makers and agent framework designers.

This can be called AEO—Agent Engine Optimization. Embedding your service so it gets selected within an agent's decision-making algorithm. This is the new marketing battlefield replacing SEO.

But this structure is far more opaque and concentrated than the SEO era. In the SEO era, at least ten search results were displayed side by side on the screen. Users had choices. But agents select only one. The form of monopoly is changing. From platform monopoly to agent model monopoly. From visible monopoly to invisible monopoly.


## Chapter 4: The Light and Shadow of "Everyone Becomes a Chairman"

Everyone becomes a chairman. It's a pleasant phrase to hear. Individuals command agent battalions and accomplish alone what previously required organizations. A one-person company rivals the productivity of a hundred-person company. This is unmistakably a narrative of democratization.

But if you look into the shadows of this metaphor, a different story appears.

There's a prerequisite to becoming a chairman: the ability to judge what to instruct agents to do—strategic decision-making capability.

Until now, "the ability to execute well" held value in the job market. Writing proposals well, coding quickly, refining design precisely. But the moment agents replace execution, only "the ability to decide what to do" holds value.

Not all humans are excellent strategists. This structure is unprecedented liberation for the few, but for the many, it's an experience of their value proposition quietly evaporating. The gap between cognitive upper and lower tiers widens dramatically.

Behind the optimism of "everyone becomes a chairman" hides the existential crisis of the majority who cannot become chairmen.


## Epilogue: The Real Question

Compressing all these trends into one sentence:

The value capture point of the digital economy is shifting from "user eyes" to "agent execution," and this transition simultaneously reorganizes interfaces, advertising, billing, platform power, and labor structures.

But at the deepest bottom of all these changes lies one question.

In a world where agents perform all executions, where exactly does the unique value of humans lie?

This wears the clothes of technological forecasting, but its essence is an ontological question.

And perhaps, only those who have an answer to this question can become chairmen in the true sense.
